Education Justice Analysis

Introduction

To address the issue of social justice and its determinants, our group conducted comprehensive exploration in the topic.[1,2,3] While contemplating various domains such as environmental protection [1], resource conservation, and human rights[2], we discerned a distinct connection to the subject of education justice[3], fueled by our identity as college students.

Although education can serve as the foundation for equality and provide communities with skills necessary to secure better employement opportunities, access to education is not equitable. Despite the inequality, education justice is a critical concept in fostering equal opportunities and promoting a fair and inclusive society. It emphasizes the need to ensure that every individual, regardless of their background, socio-economic status, or other factors, has access to high-quality education and the resources necessary to succeed. Different from other social injustices, education equality is critical since it not only affects the present but also the future generation.

The most pivotal catalyst for our focus on this matter was the recent Supreme Court's affirmative action decision during the summer[4], delineating that institutions of higher education can no longer consider race as a specific criterion in their admission processes.

Given the multifaceted nature of Education Justice, we decided to develope an interactive interface. We hope the interface would have the following 3 advantages:

1. Enhanced Comprehension: We would like to use an interactive interface uses visualization to facilitate understanding of the intricacies in Education Justice. We aim to simplify complex elements within educational systems.

2. Non-Researchers Friendly: We provide an alternative for individuals outside of academia and research to gain information on educational research results. We would like to engage with a broader audience, including policymakers, educators, and the general public.

3. Raise Public Awareness: We aim to elevate public awareness on education justice through an interactive interface. Through the power of the internet, we would like the interface to to catalyze informed discussions and foster a collective understanding on the multifaceted challenges in our education systems.

Data Collection and Sources:

We used the Opportunity Insights College Admissions Dataset. The dataset includes admissions and attendance data from US colleges along with test data. We also used CollegeBoard report for information on GPA and Standardized Examinations.

    II.I Absolute Attendance Rate

    The graph that the anticipated linear correlation in the data does not hold true. Instead, the graph displays peaks on the ends, indicating that colleges prioritize different values in their admissions criteria. This can be explained by the different situations for either extremes, individuals from wealthier backgrounds exhibit a higher enrollment percentage since they are able to afford to pay full tuition and those in lower brackets who may not be financially able often receive a favorable aid plan. However, individuals in the middle-income range receive minimal tuition assistance, although they lack the means to pay for their education in full. The right side of the graph appears more sparse, emphasizing the difference between public and private schools in these particular brackets. Public schools emerge as significant players, as they can offer more academic scholarships, influencing the decision-making process for prospective attendees of varing financial backgrounds. While the groups generally exhibit similar trends, specific examples, such as Harvard, stand out, highlighting the impact of individual school policies on admissions. This emphasizes the importance of considering unique institutional factors when interpreting broader trends in college admissions.

    II.II Ratio of Relative Attendance to Relative Application Rates

    This graph indicates a linear correlation with income, aligning with the expected trend. Schools demonstrate a cohesive flow, with the trend being more pronounced in higher-ranking institutions. However, there is a noticeable difference in absolute attendance rates across income levels within a college. The explanation for this difference can be explained by different financial situations. For example, an individuals from lower-income backgrounds is admitted, they may be less inclined to attend due to higher cost of tuition and may likely favor other more affordable options. This suggests that economic considerations play a significant role in shaping attendance choices, even when admissions policies accommodate lower-income individuals.

    V. Conclusion

    In conclusion, education justice is importance due to its profound impact on both individuals and society. Our interactive interface takes into account of indicators like application/admission/attendance rates to explore the intricate nature of education injustices. Each trend in the plot provides insight into how these injustices manifest, accompanied by our hypotheses on how these trends form.

    In the absolute attendance rate graph, peaks at the extremes imply that middle bracket students are less likely to attend, attributed to the high cost of school and insufficient financial aid. The relative attendance rate graph exhibits an anticipated linear relationship, indicating higher income brackets' increased likelihood to attend due to their ability to afford high tuition costs. The unweighted relative application graph displays a linear relationship, suggesting that higher-income bracket students may have more resources, enabling greater application numbers than lower-income students. The ratio graph reveals higher attendance rates than application numbers at both extremes, highlighting inequality between different income brackets.

    The key takeaway from this interactive interface is that college admission is influenced by multiple factors, far beyond GPA and high school ranks. This finding is also coherent with the connection of various elements contributing to education injustice.
